PROPERTY ACCESS PROBLEMS? HOW I HANDLE UNAVAILABLE HEIRS OR TENANTS

In probate real estate, access is everything. Without it, the property cannot be inspected, cleaned out, photographed, or shown to buyers. It seems simple enough: get the keys, open the door, and move forward. But in reality, access is one of the most common issues that stalls probate and trust sales before they even start.

Sometimes an heir who controls the keys is out of state or simply refuses to cooperate. Other times, tenants do not respond, or a caregiver suddenly goes silent. Executors may be ready to move forward, attorneys may be prepared with documents, but none of it matters if no one can actually get inside the property.

No access means no showings. No access means no inspections. No access means no sale. This single issue can freeze a case for weeks or even months. But it does not have to.

This is exactly where I step in. My role as a probate-trained real estate professional is to take control of these difficult situations and resolve them in a respectful, professional, and efficient way. Below is the process I follow to handle access issues without adding stress to the attorney, executor, or family.

STEP 1: I TRACK DOWN WHO HAS THE KEYS AND MAKE DIRECT CONTACT

The first step is always clarity. Too many cases stall because no one knows exactly who has the keys, where they are, or whether anyone is currently occupying the home. Executors often assume another family member has them. Attorneys sometimes assume the executor is in control. In reality, access is often scattered or unclear.

I take responsibility for solving that problem immediately. I identify who currently has control of the keys or the property itself. Then I make direct contact. I do not rely on third-hand messages or leave the responsibility on the attorney’s shoulders. Whether it is an heir, a tenant, or a family member, I reach out myself with professionalism and a clear purpose.

This alone often moves things forward. Once the right person has been identified and contacted, solutions become possible.

STEP 2: I APPROACH WITH RESPECT, NOT PRESSURE

One of the biggest reasons access becomes a problem is because people feel ignored, cornered, or disrespected. When that happens, they shut down and stop communicating. Forcing the issue only makes things worse.

I take the opposite approach. I never show up demanding cooperation. Instead, I explain what is happening with the property, how their cooperation fits into the bigger picture, and what their choices mean for the estate. By making the process clear and showing respect for their position, I turn potential conflict into cooperation.

Nine out of ten times, this approach works. A calm conversation built on respect is often enough to open the door and get the process back on track.

STEP 3: I OFFER OPTIONS FOR COORDINATED ENTRY

Not everyone is willing to simply hand over keys. That is understandable. They may not trust the process yet, or they may feel protective of the property. Instead of treating that as resistance, I offer options that make them feel secure while still allowing the estate to move forward.

This might mean scheduling a walkthrough at a specific time, accompanying them during the visit, or using a neutral third-party system like a coded lockbox. By giving them structure and choices, I reduce their hesitation and create a path to access that feels safe and professional.

The goal is not to force the issue. The goal is to remove obstacles until access becomes possible.

STEP 4: I LOOP IN THE ATTORNEY WHEN NEEDED

In some cases, no amount of communication or respect will resolve the problem. If the heir, tenant, or occupant continues to refuse access or becomes hostile, I do not escalate the situation myself. Instead, I provide the attorney with a full, documented record of what has been attempted.

This includes my outreach, their responses (or lack of responses), and the current status. By documenting everything cleanly, I give the attorney the information they need to take the next legal step, whether that means filing for a court order, eviction, or another legal remedy.

The difference is that the attorney is not left guessing or trying to track down information themselves. They get a clear, professional update that allows them to act efficiently while I continue to support the real estate side.

STEP 5: I MOVE FAST ONCE ACCESS IS GAINED

The moment access is secured, the real work begins. Probate and trust sales are often under pressure, and weeks or months may have already been lost due to access problems. I do not waste any more time.

I immediately move forward with cleanouts, professional photos, insurance coverage, inspections, and anything else required to prepare the property for listing. My goal is to make up for lost time and keep the file moving quickly now that the property is available.

By staying fully prepared ahead of time, I can execute a fast turnaround the minute we step inside. That prevents further delays and keeps the estate’s financial interests protected.

WHY ACCESS PROBLEMS SHOULD NEVER FALL ON ATTORNEYS

One of the most frustrating parts of probate real estate is that attorneys often get dragged into issues that have nothing to do with legal work. Property access is one of the most common examples.

Executors and heirs will call their attorney to complain about tenants, ask about missing keys, or demand help with uncooperative family members. None of these issues should ever fall on the attorney’s shoulders. But without a dedicated real estate professional managing access, attorneys often become the default point of contact.

That is where I make a difference. By stepping in early and managing access problems directly, I prevent attorneys from being pulled into unnecessary drama. Instead of fielding calls about keys and tenants, attorneys get clear, timely updates that keep them informed without wasting their time.

WHY EXECUTORS AND FAMILIES APPRECIATE MY ROLE

Executors are often overwhelmed, especially if they live out of town or have never handled probate before. They do not know who to call, how to approach uncooperative heirs, or how to deal with tenants who refuse to engage. That leads to stress, delays, and unnecessary conflict.

When I take ownership of the access process, executors immediately feel supported. They see that someone is handling the situation with professionalism and that progress is being made. Families appreciate that the case is moving forward without added pressure on them.

In the end, the heirs benefit most. Once access is secured, the property can be prepared and sold, allowing the estate to close faster and with less conflict.

BOTTOM LINE

Probate real estate cases stall all the time over something as basic as a locked door. Access issues may seem small, but they can delay a case for months and cost the estate thousands of dollars in lost value or extended holding costs.
