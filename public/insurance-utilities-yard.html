INSURANCE, UTILITIES, YARD: WHO’S HANDLING IT WHILE YOU WAIT?

Probate real estate is never just about the legal filings and court timelines. While attorneys are drafting documents and executors are reviewing paperwork, the property itself is sitting there. And the truth is, a property does not sit quietly. It changes every single day.

The grass grows. The water heater might start leaking. The lights stay on. Mail piles up. Insurance policies expire. Neighbors notice when nothing is being done. If no one is paying attention, what looks like a simple waiting period can quietly turn into thousands of dollars in losses and major liability for the estate.

This is one of the most overlooked areas in probate real estate. Everyone assumes the property is “fine” until it is not. By the time the problems show up, they are often expensive and can delay the entire case. That is where I come in. My role is to manage these issues before they cause damage. I make sure the home is safe, secure, and financially protected while the legal process continues.

WHY THE PROPERTY CANNOT BE IGNORED

Most executors and heirs are not local property managers. They are dealing with grief, paperwork, and family conversations. Attorneys are handling the court filings and guiding the case. That means no one is actually managing the house itself. And unfortunately, a vacant home is not neutral. It is a liability.

Without someone stepping in, the estate risks:

Losing insurance coverage because the home is vacant

Water or electrical damage that no one discovers until it is too late

Yard overgrowth that lowers curb appeal and invites code violations

Break-ins or squatters that create legal headaches

Higher carrying costs from utilities that should have been managed

Every week these issues continue, the estate loses money and value. That is why I make property oversight my first priority.

STEP ONE: I VERIFY INSURANCE IMMEDIATELY

The very first thing I check is whether the property is insured, and more importantly, whether it is insured correctly. Many families do not realize that a standard homeowner’s insurance policy often becomes invalid once the home is vacant. If that policy lapses or is no longer valid, the estate is exposed.

I confirm whether the coverage is active and whether it is structured as a vacant or estate property. Often that means securing a DP-1 or DP-3 policy, depending on the situation. I am not a financial advisor, but I know what questions to ask and which providers to connect with. This step is not optional. One fire, one break-in, or even one slip-and-fall on the property could create a lawsuit and drain the estate if insurance is not in place.

STEP TWO: I REVIEW ALL UTILITIES AND COORDINATE NEXT STEPS

Utilities are one of the easiest things to overlook. Gas, electric, water, trash, and even internet services might still be active long after they are needed. On the other hand, some services should remain on to protect the property. For example, water and electricity may be needed for inspections, cleaning, or showings.

I work with the executor to identify what should stay on, what can be paused, and what can be transferred. This saves the estate from unnecessary bills while also ensuring the property is safe and ready for market activity.

STEP THREE: I KEEP THE YARD AND EXTERIOR MAINTAINED

Nothing signals “vacant” faster than an overgrown yard. Tall weeds, untrimmed bushes, and piles of leaves make a property stand out to neighbors, city officials, and unfortunately, trespassers. I schedule yard cleanup immediately and set up recurring maintenance if needed.

This is not about creating a perfect landscape. It is about protecting curb appeal, avoiding complaints, and sending the message that the property is being managed. A well-kept exterior reduces risk and keeps potential buyers interested when the home is ready to list.

STEP FOUR: I SECURE THE PROPERTY

If the property is vacant, security becomes a priority. Vacant homes attract unwanted attention, and problems can escalate quickly. I coordinate lock changes, board windows when necessary, and set up coded access through a lockbox.

I also keep an eye on the property regularly, sending photos and updates so the executor and attorney know everything is secure. If squatters or trespassing ever become a risk, I act immediately to protect the estate. This prevents costly legal action later and gives peace of mind to everyone involved.

STEP FIVE: I COORDINATE VENDORS WITHOUT OVERLOADING THE EXECUTOR

The executor is not supposed to be a property manager. They should not have to chase contractors, schedule locksmiths, or negotiate with landscapers. That is my role. I handle vendor coordination from beginning to end.

That means:

Gathering quotes from reliable vendors

Scheduling cleanouts, repairs, and maintenance

Confirming that work is completed

Keeping everyone updated with clean, timestamped reports

Attorneys stay focused on the law. Executors stay focused on their responsibilities. I keep the property moving without creating more stress.
