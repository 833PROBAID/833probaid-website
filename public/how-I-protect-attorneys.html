HOW I PROTECT ATTORNEYS FROM GETTING DRAGGED INTO REAL ESTATE DRAMA

If you have handled probate or trust cases long enough, you have felt it.

You are hired to manage the legal side of the case. But somehow, you end up solving problems that have nothing to do with the law. Heirs start fighting over who has the keys. The executor calls you about junk removal. One sibling refuses to sign the listing paperwork. And all of it funnels into your office like you are not only the attorney, but also the real estate manager.

This is where I come in. As a probate-trained real estate professional, my job is to keep attorneys out of the day-to-day property drama and focused on their legal work. Here is how I protect you from getting pulled into the mess.

I TAKE OVER THE REAL ESTATE LANE FULLY

From the very first conversation, I set the expectation with the executor and family. I make it clear what I will be handling, and what does not need to land on the attorney’s desk anymore.

Questions about cleanouts? They come to me.

Issues with access or lockboxes? They call me.

Concerns about showings, buyers, or offers? I am their point of contact.

I set the tone, the plan, and the process from the beginning. I do not defer to the attorney unless it is a legal question. That way, you are free to do what you do best, while I keep the property moving forward.

I ESTABLISH BOUNDARIES FROM DAY ONE

One of the biggest reasons attorneys get pulled into drama is because no one else steps up to lead. Families are confused, the executor is overwhelmed, and everyone assumes the lawyer is in charge of everything.

I eliminate that confusion by establishing boundaries immediately. I explain that I am responsible for:

Coordinating cleanouts

Scheduling property access

Providing valuations

Dealing with agents and buyers

Keeping the property moving toward sale

By defining my role clearly, I keep your name out of daily issues unless absolutely necessary. This gives you breathing room to focus on the legal work instead of becoming a project manager.

I RESOLVE CONFLICTS WITH PROFESSIONAL NEUTRALITY

Real estate stirs up old family tension. One heir wants to hold the property. Another is pushing to sell quickly. Another is upset about how the process feels. Before you know it, you are being copied on heated email chains or pulled into family disputes.

That is where my experience matters.

I never take sides. I do not add to the conflict. I listen carefully, de-escalate emotions, and bring the conversation back to what needs to happen. I present clear options, supported by facts and data, not personal opinions.

This calm, neutral leadership keeps heirs from dragging the attorney into the middle as a referee. They see me as the person to manage the property side, not the lawyer.

I PROVIDE UPDATES, NOT PROBLEMS

When I communicate with attorneys, I do not come with a list of problems. I come with solutions and status updates. My role is to keep you informed, not to hand you extra work.

For example, instead of saying: “The cleanout has not been scheduled, what do we do?” I will say: “The cleanout is scheduled for Tuesday at 10am, and I will confirm completion with photos for your records.”

I make sure you know what is happening, what has been completed, and if anything may affect the legal timeline. You get clarity, not chaos.

I MAKE YOU LOOK GOOD TO YOUR CLIENTS

At the end of the day, your client does not separate the legal side from the real estate side. To them, it all feels connected.

When their property is handled smoothly, they think: “My attorney really has a great team.” That reflects positively on you. You were not just the lawyer who managed paperwork. You were the professional who brought in the right partner to move the property forward and relieve family stress.

That kind of client experience builds long-term trust, strengthens your reputation, and leads to referrals.

WHY ATTORNEYS SHOULD NOT HANDLE REAL ESTATE

Attorneys are hired to manage probate and trust law, not to act as project managers for property issues. Yet in many cases, attorneys end up fielding calls about lockboxes, showings, and cleanouts. This drains time and creates frustration.

Here are three reasons why attorneys should avoid real estate management:

IT PULLS FOCUS FROM LEGAL WORK.

Time spent chasing vendors or refereeing family disputes is time taken away from legal progress.

IT CREATES LIABILITY.

When attorneys step into tasks outside their legal role, it can create confusion or blame if something goes wrong.

IT CAUSES DELAYS.

Without someone experienced leading the property process, families often stall, waiting on the attorney to handle things they were never meant to manage.

By bringing me in, you avoid all three problems.
