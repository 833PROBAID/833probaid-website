HEIRS WANT TOP DOLLAR, BUT THE HOUSE NEEDS WORK. HERE’S WHAT I DO

One of the most common challenges in probate and trust real estate is this: the heirs want top dollar, but the house is far from market ready.

The situation plays out more often than you might think. The family is convinced the home should sell for as much as the neighbor’s or what Zillow says online. But when you step inside, the reality is different. The paint is faded. The carpet is worn. The kitchen and bathrooms are original. Years of deferred maintenance show.

This is when expectations collide with reality. If no one steps in to address it, the entire sale can fall apart.

As a probate-trained real estate professional, I specialize in handling this exact problem. My role is to help heirs understand the truth about the property, give them clear options, and make sure the estate gets the best possible outcome without delays, confusion, or conflict.

Here is how I approach these situations step by step.

STEP 1: WALK THE PROPERTY AND CALL IT LIKE IT IS

The first step is honesty. I personally walk through the home and assess the condition. I bring the heirs with me if possible and explain what I see. My approach is respectful but clear. I do not sugarcoat the situation, and I do not tell them only what they want to hear.

Instead, I explain how today’s buyers think. A buyer walking into a house that looks outdated or poorly maintained does not pay top dollar. They think about the cost of renovations, the time they need to spend, and whether their loan will even be approved after inspections.

By pointing out how condition directly impacts appraisals, loan approvals, and buyer psychology, I help the heirs understand that pricing is not based on hopes or memories. It is based on how the market will respond.

STEP 2: PRESENT TWO CLEAR OPTIONS

Once the reality is clear, I give the heirs two paths forward:

SELL AS-IS:

This means putting the house on the market without changes and pricing it according to its current condition. The estate avoids the stress of improvements, but the price will reflect the property’s flaws.

MAKE TARGETED UPGRADES:

In some cases, small, focused updates can dramatically raise the sale price. For example, a $5,000 investment in cleaning, fresh paint, and landscaping can bring in tens of thousands more in buyer interest.

I do not overwhelm the heirs with endless choices. I break it down into simple comparisons:

What the property would sell for today, untouched.

What it could sell for with a few upgrades.

The heirs can then weigh the difference in net proceeds against the estate’s timeline and budget. This approach puts them in control but gives them clarity they would not otherwise have.

STEP 3: MANAGE THE UPGRADE PROCESS WHEN CHOSEN

If the heirs decide to move forward with upgrades, I take full responsibility. I do not leave them with a to-do list or make them chase contractors. I manage everything.

I bring in trusted vendors.

I oversee the schedule.

I coordinate cleanouts, painting, repairs, or staging as needed.

I keep the heirs updated without overwhelming them.

If they choose to sell as-is instead, I support that decision completely. I adjust the marketing strategy so the property still reaches the right buyers and closes without delays. Either way, the case keeps moving forward.

STEP 4: BACK EVERY OPINION WITH DATA

Families often have emotional ties to the property. They remember holidays, milestones, and years of memories. That attachment can make it hard to accept the real condition of the home or the price it can actually sell for.

I never argue or pressure. Instead, I present data. I show comparable homes that recently sold, highlight differences in condition, and explain how buyers actually behave in today’s market.

By relying on data, not emotion, I shift the conversation from wishful thinking to informed decision-making. Heirs often calm down once they see proof instead of feeling like they are being talked down to.

STEP 5: MAKE THE ATTORNEY’S LIFE EASIER

When these conversations do not happen early, the attorney often gets dragged in. Suddenly the lawyer is the one answering value questions, mediating arguments between heirs, or trying to explain why the final sale price does not match Zillow’s estimate.

That is not the attorney’s role.

My job is to step in before those problems reach the legal team. I handle expectations, keep documentation, and take the stress off everyone’s plate. Attorneys can stay focused on the legal work while I manage the real estate side with clarity and professionalism.

WHY THIS APPROACH WORKS

Probate and trust sales rarely fall apart because of legal documents. They fall apart because heirs cannot agree, expectations are unrealistic, or no one steps up to guide the property side.

By providing clear options, honest communication, and data-backed strategies, I keep cases moving. Families feel respected. Attorneys avoid unnecessary headaches. And the estate gets a result that is realistic and fair.

A REAL EXAMPLE

In one case, the heirs were convinced the property was worth $650,000 because a neighbor had sold at that price. The problem was their home had not been updated in 40 years. The carpet was original, the kitchen was outdated, and the yard was overgrown.

I showed them the comps. In its current state, the home would likely sell for closer to $525,000. But with a $7,500 investment in cleanup, new paint, and basic landscaping, it could reasonably list for $600,000 and attract multiple offers.

The heirs agreed to the small upgrades. The result? The home sold in under two weeks for $615,000. Without that strategy, they would have been disappointed and frustrated by low offers.

THE BOTTOM LINE

Probate and trust sales do not collapse because of legal issues. They stall or fail because of unrealistic expectations and poor real estate guidance.

That is why I step in early to handle the property side. I give heirs the truth, backed by data. I show them options so they can choose the best path. And I manage the entire process so attorneys and families are not left scrambling.

If you are dealing with heirs who want top dollar but the home clearly needs work, let me take the lead. Together, we can move the case forward with clarity, confidence, and results.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

CAN YOU SELL A PROBATE PROPERTY AS-IS?

Yes, you can. Many estates choose to sell properties as-is. The key is to price it correctly so buyers see the value and the estate avoids long delays.

HOW MUCH SHOULD HEIRS SPEND ON UPGRADES BEFORE SELLING?

It depends on the property and the market. In many cases, small upgrades such as cleaning, painting, or landscaping can bring a strong return without overspending.

WHAT HAPPENS IF THE HEIRS CANNOT AGREE ON UPGRADES?

If the family cannot agree, the property can still be sold as-is. My role is to provide data and options so heirs can make informed choices, but the estate does not have to wait for total agreement to move forward.
